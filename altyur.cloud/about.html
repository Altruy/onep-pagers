Why AI automation

I spent years inside business operations before AI was a realistic option for most companies. I watched finance teams burn Monday mornings pulling numbers from five different systems just to produce a report that would be stale by the time it reached leadership. I watched sales teams lose deals not because they were outcompeted but because their follow-up process could not keep pace with their pipeline. I watched founders make strategic decisions on week-old data because the people who could produce current data were too busy producing last week's version.

The tools existed. The processes were documented. The people were capable. The connection was always missing.

When large language models matured and automation platforms became sophisticated enough to handle real-world complexity — exceptions, edge cases, legacy integrations — the opportunity became clear: not to replace business functions, but to remove the manual execution layer that was consuming the most valuable people in every organisation.

What I have learned

After 50+ production AI systems, the pattern that determines success or failure is almost never technical. It is whether the team did the process documentation work before building anything.

Most AI automation projects fail because they start with a tool and work backwards. Someone sees a demo, buys a subscription, and asks "what can we automate with this?" That is the wrong question. The right question is: "What is the actual constraint in this workflow, and what would it take to remove it?" The answer to that question determines the tool — not the other way around.

The companies that get the most from AI automation are not the ones with the biggest budgets. They are the ones willing to spend two sessions genuinely mapping their current state before touching anything. That discipline is rare. When we find it in a client, the builds almost always succeed.

What I build — and why it matters that it runs

I care about systems that run in production. That sounds obvious, but it is not. There is an enormous amount of AI "automation" that works in demos and breaks in the real world — because it does not account for exceptions, because it has no error handling, because it was built for the happy path and the real world is not the happy path.

Every system I ship has monitoring, exception routing, documentation, and a handover process. Your team should understand what was built and why. Six months after the engagement ends, you should be able to extend it yourself, or explain it to a new hire, or show it to an auditor. That is what production means to me.
